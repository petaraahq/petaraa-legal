Our Story

Built from struggle. Fuelled by purpose. Built for you.

Who Am I?

My name is Raza. I'm 20 years old. And before I tell you about Petaraa, I want to tell you about me — because Petaraa didn't come from a business plan or a whiteboard idea. It came from years of trying, failing, getting back up, and refusing to give in.

I grew up in a small town. The kind of place where opportunities don't just show up at your door. From a young age, I knew that if I wanted something different, I was going to have to go find it myself — even if it meant taking roads that didn't lead anywhere, at first.

"I didn't come from money. I didn't come from connections. I came from a decision — a decision to never stop trying, no matter how many times the answer was no."

Chapter 1 — The Dream That Said No

When I was 18, I set my eyes on Merchant Navy. The idea was powerful — discipline, structure, a life at sea, a career that felt bigger than my small town. I gave it everything I had.

For 3 months, I prepared. I studied. I appeared for online exams. I sat through interviews. I did everything that was asked of me.

And then — I failed. It wasn't close. It just didn't happen. And for a while, that hurt. Not just because I failed an exam — but because I had put my hopes into something and watched them not work out. At 18, that's a heavy thing to carry.

But here's what I learned from that moment: failing something doesn't mean you're a failure. It means that particular door was not meant for you — and somewhere else, a different door is waiting.

"That rejection from Merchant Navy was not the end of my story. It was just the universe redirecting me to something bigger."

Chapter 2 — The Team That Taught Me Everything

After Merchant Navy, I tried Digital Marketing. I learned, I experimented — but I couldn't make it click at that stage. Another chapter that taught me things, but didn't deliver the outcome I was looking for.

Then came the chapter that would change me forever. I joined Forever Living Products (FLP) — a US-based direct selling company. And I want to be very clear: I didn't just join to sell a product. I joined to build something.

Building the Team

Over the next year and more, I built a team of 16+ people. These weren't just names on a list — they were real people who I recruited, trained, mentored, and pushed to grow every single day. I ran operations like a real business. Every day had structure. Every person had targets. Every action was tracked.

The Daily Tracking System

Every single day, I would connect with my team and go through a complete review. Nothing was left unchecked:

Hot Market & Cold Market Leads — How many leads today? From friends and family (hot market) and from strangers, social media, outreach (cold market)?
Calls Made — How many people did you speak to today? Every call counted. Every number tracked.
Sessions Shown — How many people did you present the opportunity to? Day 1 presentation — how many sat down and listened?
Enrolments — Of the people who saw the session, how many joined? Every conversion was celebrated. Every miss was a lesson.
Funnel Progress — Where was each prospect in the journey? Day 1 shown? Day 2 done? Day 3 completed? Reached the Closing Session?
Closing Sessions — The final step. How many sat in the closing session today? How many made a decision?
Personal Development — Did you do your learning today? Are you growing as a person, not just as a seller? This was non-negotiable for me.

What I Taught My Team

The tracking was only half of it. The other half was training them to become better human beings — not just better salespeople. I spent hours teaching my team skills that no school ever teaches:

🗣️ How to talk to people — the right tone, the right energy, how to make someone feel heard from the very first conversation.

🚪 How to handle rejection — because in direct selling, rejection is not the exception. It's the daily reality. I trained them to face it, absorb it, and move forward.

🔄 How to face "No" — I had one line I said to my team, every single day:

"NO means Next Opportunity."

I drilled this into them. Because the difference between someone who succeeds and someone who quits is not talent — it's what they do with the word "No." I faced hundreds of rejections myself. And every single time, I chose to treat it as a step closer to a "Yes."

The Day the Team Fell Apart

For over a year, I showed up. Every day. For 16+ people. I was their trainer, their coach, their manager, their motivator. I gave them everything I had.

And then, slowly, one by one — they started leaving. Each person had their own reason. Life happened. Priorities shifted. Other paths called. The team I had poured my soul into — dissolved.

I won't pretend that didn't hurt. It did. Deeply. But when I sat with that pain and looked at it honestly, I realized something powerful:

"I had been building someone else's dream. What if I put all this energy — all this training, tracking, leadership — into something of my own?"

Chapter 3 — The Decision That Changed Everything

After the team fell apart, I made the most important decision of my life: I was going to build my own company. Not work for someone else's vision. Mine. From scratch. On my own terms.

I started teaching myself technology. I built affiliate websites that generated real money. I learned how online businesses work from the inside out. And while I was doing all of this — I kept running into the same frustrating problem, over and over again.

Chapter 4 — The Frustration That Became a Vision

Every time I tried to grow — as a creator, as a freelancer, as someone building skills and trying to earn — I had to use five different apps:

📺 YouTube — to learn something new

💼 LinkedIn — to network and find opportunities

🛠️ Fiverr / Upwork — to find freelance work

📋 Internshala / Naukri — to find a job

📱 Instagram / Discord — to build a community

Zero connection between any of them.

Build an audience on one platform. Start from zero on another. Pay for a course here. Pay for premium there. Apply for a job somewhere else entirely. And Gen Z — my generation — doesn't want to manage five different apps. They don't want to rebuild their identity from scratch on every platform. They want one place that actually understands them.

I searched for that platform. I tried everything. Nothing existed.

"I had spent a year training people to never give up. Now it was time to apply those lessons to myself — and build what didn't exist."

Why Petaraa?

Petaraa is the platform I wish had existed when I started.

One place to learn, earn, connect, create, and grow — without switching apps, without starting over, without paying ten different platforms just to build one career.

The name Petaraa reflects a spark — the first light before something big. That's exactly what this is. The beginning of something much larger.

I'm building Petaraa for every student who doesn't have opportunities around them. For every creator who wants to earn from their skills. For every freelancer tired of platforms that take too much. For every person who has heard "No" — and chose to treat it as "Next Opportunity."

Right Now

Petaraa is being built by me — alone. No big team. No investors. No fancy office. Just me, late nights, a clear vision, and everything I've learned from years of trying and failing and getting back up.

Is it hard? Yes. Is there doubt sometimes? Of course. But I've faced harder things. I've built teams. I've handled rejection. I've watched things fall apart and started over from zero. This time, I'm building something that lasts.
